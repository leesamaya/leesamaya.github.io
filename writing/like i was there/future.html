Do you miss the future?

When I was seven I wanted a bejewelled, dark pink Nokia flip phone. By the time I was old enough for a phone, it didn't exist anymore. That small disappointment turned out to be a preview. I grew up inside a 2000s imagination that promised more than banality — Kim Possible, the iPod Nano, young adult dystopias that still had ambition even at their bleakest. The future was fruitful. What arrived instead wasn't the ordinary loss of a child's rose-coloured glasses; it was something structural. I felt it as a kid sticking my head out of the car window at night, hair against empty suburban streets — houses set down like something planted to house workers near a mall, neighbours who didn't know each other. I told my parents I felt lonely and they were confused, because there was nothing there to be lonely about. But it wasn't a loneliness that belonged only to me. It was bigger than me, and it was real. Mark Fisher calls this "the shocking difference between what we thought might have happened and what actually happened." He believed that feeling like this could become collective — that naming a shared, unexplainable ache is itself the beginning of consciousness, and that consciousness is contagious. This playlist is the soundtrack to that car ride. Bright, plastic, euphoric, suspended — a future that got cancelled somewhere along the way.
